# Statistical Arbitrage: An Empirical Test of Pairs Trading on US Large-Cap Equities
This project implements and evaluates a pairs trading strategy on liquid US large-cap equities. The strategy was tested across multiple pairs, multiple sample windows, and at the single-pair and portfolio level. The central finding is that the strategy produced no durable edge once realistic transaction costs were applied. Two intermediate results initially appeared profitable; upon further investigation, the first was attributable to the near-zero transaction cost assumption, and the second was attributable to a single market dislocation during COVID rather than a repeatable statistical relationship.
Strategy Overview
Pairs trading is premised on cointegration: two price series whose linear combination, the spread Price_A − β·Price_B, is stationary and mean-reverting. When the spread deviates significantly from its historical mean, the strategy takes a market-neutral position betting on reversion, shorting the relatively overpriced leg and buying the relatively underpriced leg. The implementation follows the standard methodology: the Engle-Granger test for cointegration, ordinary least squares regression for the hedge ratio β, and a 30-day rolling z-score of the spread as the trading signal. Positions are entered when |z| exceeds 2.0, exited when z returns to 0, and stopped out when |z| exceeds 3.5, with equal dollar amounts allocated to each leg.


A primary design consideration was the elimination of look-ahead bias, as the most common backtesting errors inflate results rather than producing visible failures. The following controls were implemented.
The z-score is computed using a 30-day rolling mean and standard deviation rather than full-sample statistics. Normalizing against the full-sample mean would define the level to which the spread reverts using future data, causing entry signals to align with realized reversions that could not have been predicted in real time.
Positions are shifted forward by one trading day prior to the computation of returns. The signal is derived from the current day's closing price, and consequently the earliest executable trade occurs on the following day.
The hedge ratio is estimated on the first 60% of observations only. In the pipeline implementation, pair selection is likewise restricted to the training window, as selecting pairs on full-sample statistics would partially select them on the basis of performance within the evaluation period itself.
Transaction costs are modeled at 20 basis points per position change, an approximation of the combined bid-ask spread and slippage incurred across both legs by a retail participant. This parameter proved to be the decisive factor in the single-pair results.
The screener ranks candidate pairs by the half-life of mean reversion, estimated from an AR(1) regression of spread changes on lagged spread levels. Reversion speed determines whether the gross profit per trade is sufficient to exceed fixed transaction costs, and was therefore prioritized over the cointegration p-value as the ranking criterion.
Results
Single Pair: KO/PEP
The cointegration of (KO, PEP) resulted in a p-value of approximately .065 in both regression directions. For the purposes of hypothesis testing, the alpha was set at .05. Any p-value below alpha signifies strong evidence of cointegration between the two observed tickers. The observed p-value of .065, while above alpha, remains a borderline statistical indicator of cointegration, and the pair was carried forward for evaluation with this limitation documented. The backtest was conducted over approximately 19 years of historical data with the hedge ratio fit on the training window only.

Under near-zero costs, a modest edge is observed. Under realistic costs, the out-of-sample Sharpe ratio of 0.15 is statistically indistinguishable from zero. Notably, the win rate declined from 59% to 41% upon the introduction of costs, indicating that the majority of profitable trades were small reversions whose gross profit did not exceed the bid-ask spread. An additional backtest conducted on 2014–2019 data, excluding the COVID period entirely, produced a negative out-of-sample return of −3.8%, ruling out the COVID dislocation as the cause of the result.
Universe Screening and Spurious Cointegration
A universe of 17 tickers spanning consumer staples, payments, energy, banking, and gold ETFs was screened, producing 136 candidate pairs. The initial screening output exhibited a notable artifact: Citigroup (C) returned p-values of approximately .0000 against nearly every ticker in the universe, including gold ETFs with which it shares no economic relationship and exhibits near-zero return correlation. Inspection of the hedge ratios revealed values near zero for these pairs. When β approaches zero, the spread y − βx degenerates to the single series y, and the cointegration test reduces to a test of whether Citigroup alone was stationary over the observed window. The apparent pairwise relationships were therefore artifacts of one range-bound stock rather than genuine cointegration.
Two additional filters were imposed in response: a minimum absolute return correlation of 0.5 and a minimum absolute hedge ratio of 0.05. These filters removed the spurious pairs and retained five economically coherent candidates on the 2015–2024 window: V/MA, CL/PG, MDLZ/PG, KMB/MDLZ, and KO/PEP. An important limitation to note is that testing 136 pairs at an alpha of .05 is expected to produce several false positives by chance alone, and consequently a favorable screening p-value was treated as grounds for further evaluation rather than as confirmation of a tradeable relationship.
Pipeline-Selected Pair: KMB/MDLZ
On one sample window, the pipeline-selected pair KMB/MDLZ produced an out-of-sample return of +78% with a Sharpe ratio of 0.72. Two characteristics of this result warranted skepticism. First, the out-of-sample Sharpe ratio substantially exceeded the in-sample Sharpe ratio of approximately 0.21. A strategy cannot conditionally improve at the train/test boundary, and consequently an out-of-sample result that materially exceeds its in-sample counterpart is more plausibly explained by favorable regime conditions within the evaluation window than by durable predictive skill. Second, the result was achieved with a win rate below 50%, indicating that a small number of large trades accounted for the majority of the return, a profile consistent with isolated tail events rather than a consistent edge.

The diversification mechanics performed as theoretically predicted. The average pairwise return correlation of 0.03 indicates near-complete independence among the pairs, and the portfolio Sharpe ratio of 0.71 exceeded the individual pair average of 0.27, with the maximum drawdown compressing to −6.3%. The blending methodology was validated separately on synthetic data containing planted cointegrated pairs, where three uncorrelated edges combined to approximately the √N multiple of the average individual Sharpe ratio predicted by portfolio theory.
However, two diagnostics undermined the headline result. The leave-one-out configuration demonstrated that KMB/MDLZ accounted for approximately 90% of the portfolio's return; the remaining four pairs, blended, produced approximately 3% over 3.6 years. Furthermore, re-running the identical basket on the 2012–2019 window, which excludes the COVID period, inverted the portfolio Sharpe ratio from +0.71 to −0.67, with KMB/MDLZ individually declining from +54.3% to −2.4%.
The mechanism underlying the original result is as follows. In the 2015–2024 configuration, the March 2020 dislocation falls near the end of the training window. Spreads widened severely during the dislocation, and the test period consequently opened with spreads in a stretched state, spending late 2020 and 2021 reverting to historical norms. The strategy therefore entered the evaluation period with a substantial one-time reversion already in place. The observed out-of-sample edge was the resolution of a single market crisis rather than a repeatable statistical relationship, and its removal from the sample eliminated, and indeed reversed, the result.
Conclusion
Across all configurations tested, the strategy produced returns that were flat to slightly negative net of 20 basis point costs, with the sole exception traced to the COVID dislocation. This outcome is consistent with economic theory rather than indicative of an insufficiently sophisticated model. Pairs trading on liquid large-cap equities is among the most widely deployed quantitative strategies in existence. Institutional participants executing this strategy incur costs of fractions of a basis point and trade at intraday frequency, competing residual mispricings down to their own cost level. The edge remaining after this competition is, by construction, smaller than the frictions faced by a retail participant. The observed result of approximately zero net of costs therefore represents the equilibrium return for a marginal participant, consistent with the limits-to-arbitrage framework and with the documented decay of pairs trading profitability since the sample periods studied in the original academic literature (Gatev, Goetzmann & Rouwenhorst, 2006).
The methodological findings are of equal significance. Each profitable-appearing result was traced to a mundane explanation through a specific diagnostic: cost sensitivity analysis for KO/PEP, train/test comparison and win-rate decomposition for KMB/MDLZ, leave-one-out analysis for the portfolio, and a COVID-excluded sample window for the final determination. Notably, an out-of-sample result that exceeds its in-sample counterpart proved to be as reliable an indicator of a spurious result as the conventional overfitting pattern in which in-sample performance fails to generalize.
Limitations and Future Work
Several limitations should be noted. The backtester allocates equal dollar amounts to each leg rather than weighting by the hedge ratio. Transaction costs are modeled as a flat per-trade charge rather than through an explicit slippage model. The hedge ratio is held fixed at its training-window estimate; a Kalman filter permitting the hedge ratio to evolve over time is the natural next implementation. The single chronological train/test split could be strengthened through walk-forward validation. Additionally, the ticker universe was selected with hindsight and is therefore subject to survivorship bias, as it contains only firms known to have survived the full sample period.
The most promising direction for future work concerns universe selection rather than model refinement. Less efficiently priced market segments, including smaller-capitalization equities and less-covered markets, plausibly retain edges accessible to non-institutional participants. An important caveat is that these segments carry proportionally higher transaction costs, and consequently the friction problem documented in this project would persist in a different form.
